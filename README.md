# AI-Native Business Design

**A practical, prompt-rich guide to planning and building AI-native products.**

*Business-first · No coding background required*

This is a working guide — not a course, not a set of slides. It is a collection of instructions, practices, and ready-to-copy **prompt templates** for taking a business idea to a working product with AI: thinking clearly about *what* to build, planning it so an AI can build it well, and then building it fast. Work through it at your own pace, and return to the prompts whenever you need them.

The examples and prompts are written for **Lovable**, but the principles apply to any modern AI build platform — Bolt, v0, Replit, Cursor, Claude Code, and others. Where something is Lovable-specific, adapt it to the tool you use.

---

## Who this is for

Founders and early teams working on a startup case — technical or not. In the AI era you no longer need to write code to build, but you do need to think clearly and describe what you want precisely. This guide is built around that skill.

---

## How to use this guide

Three chapters, best read in order the first time and used as a reference afterwards:

- **[01-mindset.md](01-mindset.md) — The AI-Native Mindset.** Seeing AI as a new way to build a company, not just a faster tool: closed loops, agents, and where humans actually add value.
- **[02-planning.md](02-planning.md) — Planning in the AI Era.** Turning a business idea into a PRD an AI can build from. Prompt-rich.
- **[03-building.md](03-building.md) — Building: Vibe Coding in Practice.** Going from a plan to a working prototype, with the everyday loop of prompting, fixing, and releasing. Prompt-rich.

Throughout, prompts appear in grey boxes. **They are templates:** replace anything in `[square brackets]` with your own project, and never run a prompt that still contains example wording. Read each one before you send it.

> **What is Markdown (.md)?** Markdown is a simple way to write formatted text with plain characters: `#` for headings, `-` for bullet points, and so on. These files are written in it, and it is worth learning to read and write — it is the format AI tools understand best.

## Before you start: set up two things

### 1. An AI thinking partner

Open the AI tool you already use — **ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini** — and make sure it understands your business. The cleanest way is a **project**: a workspace that keeps your documents and context across many conversations, so you never have to re-explain your business. Create one and add the key documents about your startup — your pitch, business plan, customer notes, anything describing what you are building and for whom.

> **Why this matters:** throughout the guide you ask the AI to reason about *your* case, not a generic one. The quality of every answer depends on how well the tool understands your business.

### 2. A build tool

Take **Lovable** into use. Lovable turns a written description of an app into a working web prototype — you describe what you want in plain language and it builds the interface and basic functionality. New users get **100 free credits** (credits are what it spends each time it builds or changes something for you). If you already have a Pro plan, create a **new workspace** for a clean start. You use Lovable in the Building chapter. Prefer another platform? The Building chapter's prompts adapt directly.

## Make it your own (optional)

You can turn this generic guide into one tailored to your business. Give the guide **and** your own startup documents to your AI tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Lovable, NotebookLM, or similar), then paste the prompt below.

> **Tip:** give the AI both the link to this repository (so it can read the guide) and your own startup documents (so it can make the output specific to you).

```
You are my business mentor, focused on moving my own business forward. You
bring a modern startup-building perspective — the kind of thinking you would
get from a top accelerator and experienced early-stage investors.

My task: I am working through an AI-native business design guide and I want to
get the full value out of it for my own business. I am giving you the guide
(a link to its GitHub repository, which you can read) and documents describing
my own startup case. Based on both, produce two things.

1) A PERSONALISED COMPANION to the guide. Organise the guide's content and flow
   so I can follow it against my own business. Do not add new content or leave
   out anything essential. Under each theme, add a section called "What could
   this mean for my business?" that raises the angles genuinely relevant to my
   case, plus a few prompting questions designed to make me think.

2) A "MY FOCUS POINTS" document. Analyse the guide's themes against my startup
   documentation and identify at least 10 focus points that are truly
   meaningful for my case — a business opportunity, an area to apply, something
   to learn, a risk, a weakness, or a new perspective. Each focus point gets a
   one-sentence title and a 1-2 sentence note on why it matters for my
   business. The goal is to show me which parts of the guide matter most for my
   startup right now, and which matter less.

Write as a skilled, experienced business mentor. Keep it clear and easy to read.
```
